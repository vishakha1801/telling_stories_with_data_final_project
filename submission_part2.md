The primary audience for this project is **adult LEGO fans and design-curious readers** ‚Äî people who have emotional familiarity with LEGO but may not have deeply reflected on how the brand has changed. This includes AFOLs, nostalgic builders, visual storytelling enthusiasts, and readers interested in cultural brand evolution.

The goal is not to judge LEGO‚Äôs transformation as positive or negative, but to make the shift *visible and legible*. By tracing how LEGO moved from small, modular toys to large-scale, display-oriented collectibles, the story invites readers to reflect on their own relationship with LEGO and ask:

### Section 1: The Era of Standardization (1950‚Äì1990)

This section establishes LEGO as a restrained, modular system designed to emphasize compatibility and imagination. Sets like *236 Garage with Automatic Door* (1956) and early Town Plan vehicles are intentionally small, formulaic, and limited in color. Even with innovations such as early Technic gears, LEGO maintains low complexity to prioritize creativity over structure.

**Visual & Narrative Intent:**
To make visible the long period where LEGO‚Äôs identity was rooted in restraint and modular freedom ‚Äî not scale.

### Section 2: The Complexity Shift (1995‚Äì2010)

## üë• User Research Protocol

### Research Goal

To evaluate whether the narrative clearly communicates LEGO‚Äôs evolution and whether readers feel emotionally grounded in both the visuals and examples.

### Target Audience

Casual LEGO fans and design-interested readers aged 20‚Äì35, familiar with LEGO but not necessarily experts.

### Participant Selection

Three individuals were selected through peer networks, representing a mix of nostalgic LEGO users and casual observers.

### Interview Script

1. What is the main story you think this is trying to tell?
2. Which visuals feel most clear or unclear?
3. Do the examples of specific LEGO sets help you understand the trends?
4. What would make this story more engaging or meaningful?

## üí¨ User Research Findings

Participants universally understood the broader narrative but expressed a desire for stronger emotional grounding through recognizable sets and visual nostalgia.

Key observations:

* Specific LEGO set references significantly increased engagement
* Clear audience framing made the story feel more personal and intentional
* Visual context worked best when paired with iconic cultural examples

Notable quotes:

> ‚ÄúI like knowing what changed, but I want to see the actual boxes I remember ‚Äî that‚Äôs what makes it hit.‚Äù
